Investment Basics - Frequently Asked Questions

1. What is an investment?
An investment is an asset or item acquired with the goal of generating income or appreciation over time. Investments can include stocks, bonds, mutual funds, real estate, and other financial instruments. The primary purpose of investing is to put money to work in order to grow wealth and achieve financial goals such as retirement, education funding, or building an emergency fund.

2. What is the difference between stocks and bonds?
Stocks represent ownership shares in a company. When you buy a stock, you become a partial owner of that company and may benefit from its growth through price appreciation and dividends. Stocks are generally considered higher-risk but offer higher potential returns over the long term.

Bonds are debt instruments issued by governments or corporations. When you buy a bond, you are essentially lending money to the issuer in exchange for periodic interest payments and the return of the principal amount at maturity. Bonds are generally considered lower-risk than stocks but typically offer lower returns.

3. What is diversification?
Diversification is an investment strategy that involves spreading investments across various asset classes, sectors, and geographic regions to reduce risk. The principle behind diversification is that a portfolio of different investments will, on average, yield higher returns and pose a lower risk than any individual investment within the portfolio. A well-diversified portfolio typically includes a mix of stocks, bonds, real estate, and other assets.

4. What is a mutual fund?
A mutual fund is a pooled investment vehicle managed by a professional fund manager. It collects money from multiple investors and invests it in a diversified portfolio of stocks, bonds, or other securities. Mutual funds offer investors the benefits of diversification, professional management, and liquidity. Each investor owns shares in the mutual fund, which represent a portion of the fund's holdings.

5. What is an Exchange-Traded Fund (ETF)?
An Exchange-Traded Fund (ETF) is a type of investment fund that trades on stock exchanges, similar to individual stocks. ETFs hold a collection of assets such as stocks, bonds, or commodities and typically track a specific index like the S&P 500 or NIFTY 50. ETFs offer lower expense ratios compared to mutual funds, tax efficiency, and the flexibility to buy and sell throughout the trading day at market prices.

6. What is the difference between active and passive investing?
Active investing involves a hands-on approach where a fund manager or investor actively selects securities, times the market, and makes frequent trades to outperform a benchmark index. Active management typically comes with higher fees due to research and trading costs.

Passive investing involves tracking a market index or benchmark with minimal trading. Index funds and ETFs are common passive investment vehicles. Passive investing generally has lower fees, lower turnover, and has historically outperformed many active strategies over the long term.

7. What is compound interest?
Compound interest is the interest calculated on both the initial principal and the accumulated interest from previous periods. It is often described as "interest on interest" and is a powerful concept in investing. For example, if you invest Rs. 10,000 at an annual interest rate of 10%, you earn Rs. 1,000 in the first year. In the second year, you earn interest on Rs. 11,000 (original principal plus first year's interest), resulting in Rs. 1,100 of interest. Over time, compound interest can significantly accelerate wealth growth.

8. What is a Systematic Investment Plan (SIP)?
A Systematic Investment Plan (SIP) is an investment method that allows investors to invest a fixed amount regularly (monthly, quarterly) in a mutual fund scheme. SIP helps in rupee cost averaging, where more units are purchased when prices are low and fewer units when prices are high. SIP also instills financial discipline and is ideal for long-term wealth creation. The minimum SIP amount can be as low as Rs. 500 per month in many mutual fund schemes.

9. What are the different types of risk in investing?
There are several types of investment risk:
- Market Risk: The risk of losses due to overall market movements.
- Credit Risk: The risk that a bond issuer may default on interest or principal payments.
- Liquidity Risk: The risk of not being able to sell an investment quickly at a fair price.
- Inflation Risk: The risk that investment returns may not keep pace with inflation, reducing purchasing power.
- Interest Rate Risk: The risk that changes in interest rates will affect bond prices inversely.
- Currency Risk: The risk of losses due to fluctuations in foreign exchange rates.
- Concentration Risk: The risk of having too much invested in a single asset or sector.

10. What is asset allocation?
Asset allocation is the strategy of dividing an investment portfolio among different asset categories such as equities, fixed income, real estate, gold, and cash equivalents. The goal of asset allocation is to balance risk and reward according to an investor's financial goals, risk tolerance, and investment horizon. A common rule of thumb is that the equity allocation percentage should be approximately 100 minus the investor's age, though this varies based on individual circumstances.
