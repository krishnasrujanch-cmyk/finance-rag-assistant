Stock Market Basics and Trading Guide

Section 1: What is the Stock Market?

The stock market is a collection of exchanges where shares of publicly listed companies are bought and sold. In India, the two main stock exchanges are the Bombay Stock Exchange (BSE), established in 1875, and the National Stock Exchange (NSE), established in 1992. The BSE's benchmark index is the Sensex (comprising 30 companies), and the NSE's benchmark index is the NIFTY 50 (comprising 50 companies).

The stock market serves as a platform for companies to raise capital by issuing shares to the public through an Initial Public Offering (IPO). After the IPO, shares are traded in the secondary market among investors. The stock market plays a crucial role in the economy by facilitating capital formation, providing liquidity, and enabling price discovery.

Section 2: How to Start Investing in Stocks

To invest in the Indian stock market, you need the following:

1. Demat Account: A dematerialized account that holds shares and securities in electronic form. It is mandatory for trading in the Indian stock market. Demat accounts are opened with Depository Participants (DPs) registered with NSDL or CDSL.

2. Trading Account: An account linked to your demat account that allows you to place buy and sell orders on the stock exchange. It acts as an intermediary between your bank account and demat account.

3. Bank Account: A savings bank account linked to your trading and demat account for fund transfers.

4. PAN Card: Permanent Account Number is mandatory for all stock market transactions and KYC verification.

5. KYC Verification: Complete Know Your Customer documentation including identity proof, address proof, and income proof.

The account opening process has been simplified with most brokers offering online account opening through e-KYC using Aadhaar-based verification, which can be completed within 15-30 minutes.

Section 3: Types of Stock Market Orders

1. Market Order: An order to buy or sell a stock immediately at the best available current price. Market orders guarantee execution but not the exact price.

2. Limit Order: An order to buy or sell a stock at a specific price or better. A buy limit order will execute at the limit price or lower, while a sell limit order will execute at the limit price or higher. Limit orders are not guaranteed to execute if the market price doesn't reach the specified limit.

3. Stop-Loss Order: An order to sell a stock when it reaches a particular price, designed to limit an investor's loss on a position. For example, setting a stop-loss at 10% below the purchase price limits the potential loss to 10%.

4. Cover Order: A market order with a compulsory stop-loss order. It provides higher leverage as the risk is limited by the stop-loss.

5. Bracket Order: An order with a target price and stop-loss price along with the entry order. It automatically places a take-profit and stop-loss order once the entry order is executed.

6. After Market Order (AMO): Orders placed outside of regular trading hours (9:15 AM to 3:30 PM IST). AMOs are queued and executed when the market opens on the next trading day.

Section 4: Fundamental Analysis

Fundamental analysis involves evaluating a company's financial statements, management quality, competitive advantages, and industry position to determine its intrinsic value. Key metrics include:

1. Price-to-Earnings Ratio (P/E): Calculated as Market Price per Share / Earnings per Share. A lower P/E may indicate an undervalued stock, while a higher P/E may indicate an overvalued stock or high growth expectations. The average P/E ratio for NIFTY 50 historically ranges between 18-22.

2. Price-to-Book Ratio (P/B): Calculated as Market Price per Share / Book Value per Share. A P/B ratio below 1 may indicate the stock is undervalued relative to its assets.

3. Debt-to-Equity Ratio (D/E): Calculated as Total Debt / Total Shareholders' Equity. A lower D/E ratio indicates a company is using less leverage and has a stronger equity position. A D/E ratio below 1 is generally considered healthy.

4. Return on Equity (ROE): Calculated as Net Income / Shareholders' Equity. ROE measures a company's profitability relative to its equity. An ROE above 15% is generally considered good.

5. Earnings Per Share (EPS): Calculated as Net Income / Total Outstanding Shares. EPS indicates a company's profitability on a per-share basis.

6. Dividend Yield: Calculated as Annual Dividend per Share / Market Price per Share. Dividend yield indicates the return an investor receives from dividends alone.

Section 5: Technical Analysis

Technical analysis involves studying historical price patterns, volume data, and chart patterns to predict future price movements. Key concepts include:

1. Support and Resistance: Support is a price level where buying pressure exceeds selling pressure, causing the price to bounce back up. Resistance is a price level where selling pressure exceeds buying pressure, causing the price to reverse downward.

2. Moving Averages: Statistical calculations that smooth out price data to identify trends. The 50-day and 200-day Simple Moving Averages (SMA) are commonly used. A "Golden Cross" occurs when the 50-day SMA crosses above the 200-day SMA (bullish signal), while a "Death Cross" occurs when the 50-day SMA crosses below the 200-day SMA (bearish signal).

3. RSI (Relative Strength Index): A momentum oscillator ranging from 0 to 100. RSI above 70 indicates overbought conditions (potential sell signal), while RSI below 30 indicates oversold conditions (potential buy signal).

4. MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence): A trend-following momentum indicator that shows the relationship between two moving averages. MACD crossovers and divergences provide buy and sell signals.

5. Volume Analysis: Trading volume confirms price trends. Rising prices with increasing volume indicate a strong uptrend, while rising prices with decreasing volume may indicate a weakening trend.

Section 6: Trading Segments and Timings

1. Equity Segment: Regular trading hours are 9:15 AM to 3:30 PM IST on weekdays (Monday to Friday), excluding stock exchange holidays. Pre-market session runs from 9:00 AM to 9:15 AM.

2. Derivatives Segment (Futures & Options): Same trading hours as equity segment. Derivative contracts have monthly and weekly expiry dates.

3. Currency Segment: Trading hours are 9:00 AM to 5:00 PM IST.

4. Commodity Segment: Trading hours vary; agricultural commodities trade from 10:00 AM to 5:00 PM, while non-agricultural commodities (gold, silver, crude oil) trade from 9:00 AM to 11:30 PM IST.

Section 7: Settlement Cycle

The Indian stock market follows a T+1 settlement cycle, meaning trades are settled one business day after the transaction date. When you buy shares on Monday, they will be credited to your demat account by Tuesday. When you sell shares, the sale proceeds will be credited to your bank account by the next business day.

Section 8: Important Regulatory Bodies

1. SEBI (Securities and Exchange Board of India): The primary regulatory authority for the securities market in India. SEBI was established in 1992 and is responsible for protecting investor interests, regulating the securities market, and promoting market development.

2. RBI (Reserve Bank of India): Regulates monetary policy, foreign exchange, and banking operations. RBI's interest rate decisions significantly impact the stock market.

3. NSDL and CDSL: National Securities Depository Limited and Central Depository Services Limited are the two depositories that hold securities in electronic form.

Section 9: Investor Protection and Grievances

Investors can file complaints through the following channels:
- SEBI SCORES (SEBI Complaint Redress System): An online platform for filing and tracking complaints against listed companies and intermediaries.
- Stock Exchange Investor Grievance Cells: BSE and NSE have dedicated investor grievance resolution mechanisms.
- Investor Protection Fund: A fund maintained by stock exchanges to compensate investors in case of default by trading members.

Important Note: Stock market investments are subject to market risks. Investors should conduct thorough research or consult a SEBI-registered investment advisor before making investment decisions. Past performance of any security does not guarantee future results.
