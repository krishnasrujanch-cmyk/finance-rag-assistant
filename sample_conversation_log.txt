You: What are the different types of mutual funds?

Assistant: Based on the Mutual Fund Investment Guide, there are several types of
mutual funds:

1. Equity Mutual Funds - Invest primarily in stocks. Includes Large-Cap Funds
   (top 100 companies), Mid-Cap Funds (101st to 250th), Small-Cap Funds (251st
   and below), Multi-Cap Funds, and Flexi-Cap Funds.

2. Debt Mutual Funds - Invest in fixed-income securities like government bonds,
   corporate bonds, and treasury bills. Types include Liquid Funds, Short Duration
   Funds, Corporate Bond Funds, Gilt Funds, and Dynamic Bond Funds.

3. Hybrid Mutual Funds - Invest in a mix of equity and debt. Includes Conservative
   Hybrid (10-25% equity), Balanced Advantage, and Aggressive Hybrid (65-80% equity).

4. Index Funds - Passively managed funds that replicate a specific market index like
   NIFTY 50 or Sensex with low expense ratios (0.05% to 0.50%).

5. ELSS (Equity Linked Savings Scheme) - Tax-saving funds with Section 80C deduction
   up to Rs. 1,50,000 per year and a 3-year lock-in period.
  Sources: mutual_fund_guide.txt

You: How do I start investing in stocks?

Assistant: According to the Stock Market Basics guide, to invest in the Indian stock
market you need the following:

1. Demat Account - A dematerialized account that holds shares in electronic form.
   It is mandatory for trading. Opened with Depository Participants registered with
   NSDL or CDSL.

2. Trading Account - Linked to your demat account, allows you to place buy and sell
   orders on the stock exchange.

3. Bank Account - A savings account linked to your trading and demat account for
   fund transfers.

4. PAN Card - Mandatory for all stock market transactions and KYC verification.

5. KYC Verification - Including identity proof, address proof, and income proof.

The account opening process has been simplified with most brokers offering online
opening through e-KYC using Aadhaar-based verification, which can be completed
within 15-30 minutes.

Important Note: Stock market investments are subject to market risks. Investors
should conduct thorough research or consult a SEBI-registered investment advisor.
  Sources: stock_market_basics.txt

You: Tell me about PPF.

Assistant: Based on the documents, the Public Provident Fund (PPF) is a long-term
savings scheme backed by the Government of India. Here are the key features:

- Lock-in Period: 15 years, extendable in blocks of 5 years.
- Current Interest Rate: Approximately 7.1% per annum, compounded annually.
  The rate is revised quarterly by the government.
- Minimum Deposit: Rs. 500 per year.
- Maximum Deposit: Rs. 1,50,000 per year.
- Tax Treatment: EEE (Exempt-Exempt-Exempt) status - contributions qualify for
  Section 80C deduction, interest earned is tax-free, and maturity proceeds are
  tax-free.
- Partial Withdrawal: Allowed from the 7th financial year onwards, up to 50% of
  the balance at the end of the 4th preceding year.
- Loan Against PPF: Available from the 3rd year to the 6th year, up to 25% of
  the balance at the end of the 2nd preceding year.

PPF is one of the safest investment options available in India.
  Sources: fixed_deposits_savings.txt, personal_finance_planning.txt
